Key Findings:

Prevalence of Stunting Among Children Under Five by Age Group

Key Findings:

  • Overall, the prevalence of stunting decreased from 37.9% in 2015 to 33.1% in 2020.
  • A significant reduction in stunting was observed among children aged 12-17 months (8.8 percentage points) and 18-23 months (10.4 percentage points).
  • Among children under 6 months and those aged 6-8 months, the prevalence of stunting increased from 2015 to 2020.
  • 16.2% of children less than 6 months were already stunted.
  • Stunting increased with the age of the child up to 35 months and then decreased as the child got older.
  • Stunting rose to 32.8% at 12-17 months and peaked at 40.4% at 24-35 months.

prevalence of stunting among children under five by province

Key Findings:

  • The Northern province had shown a slight increase in the prevalence of stunting between 2015 and 2020 .
  • The prevalence of stunting was highest in Western and Northern provinces
  • Among children under 6 months and those aged 6-8 months, the prevalence of stunting increased from 2015 to 2020.
  • Stunting reduction had happened in the Southern (7.8% points), Eastern and Western provinces between 2015 and 2020

prevalence of stunting among children under five by sex

Key Findings:

  • Boys tend to be more stunted than girls
  • Stunting reduction had happened more in boys (5.7% points) than in girls between 2015 and 2020

prevalence of stunting by size at birth

Key Findings:

  • Children were more likely to be stunted if they were born smaller
  • Stunting reduction had happened more in the children born small (5.2% points) or average or larger (5% points) than born very small between 2015 and 2020
  • Children were more likely to be stunted if they were born smaller

prevalence of stunting by wealth quintile

Key Findings:

  • Children were more likely to be stunted if their families were poorer
  • Poverty alleviation plays a significant role for stunting reduction

Stunting among U5 Children in City of Kigali

(DHS 2015 - DHS 2019/20)

Key Findings:

  • Improved access to healthcare is indicated by decreased distances to facilities for families.
  • Increased satisfaction with healthcare quality correlates with better health outcomes for children.
  • Fewer emergency room visits suggest enhanced preventive care and timely medical attention.
  • More consistent follow-up care is linked to effective monitoring of child growth and development.
  • Overall healthcare improvements contribute significantly to stunting prevention in children.
